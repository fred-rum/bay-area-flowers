clarkia

Clarkia spp.
Member of evening primrose family (family Onagraceae)

Except for old historical records and extremely rare plants that I don't expect to encounter, there are no other species of this genus in the bay area.

Key features:

  • corolla shape and color distribution
  • buds pendant or not
  • all 4 sepals staying fused or not
  • ovary 4-grooved or 8-grooved

Unique corolla shapes

elegant clarkia
Clarkia unguiculata
elegant clarkia
Clarkia unguiculata
  • axis in bud straight; buds reflexed [i.e. drooping]
  • all 4 sepals staying fused [theoretically, but this varies]
  • petals 10–25 mm [unspotted]; petal claw slender [unlobed]
  • found everywhere
photo
 diamond clarkia
Clarkia rhomboidea
diamond clarkia
Clarkia rhomboidea
  • looks much like elegant clarkia
  • axis in bud recurved at tip; buds pendent
  • sepals all coming free [sometimes reflexed, sometimes spreading]
  • petals 6–12 mm, generally spotted; petal claw broad, 2-lobed [lobes are slight bumps on either side of the base of the claw]
  • found at Sierra Azul

Standard corolla shapes, buds erect or reflexed

winecup clarkia
Clarkia purpurea
winecup clarkia
Clarkia purpurea
  • sepals all coming free or staying fused in pairs [occasionally all 4 fused]
  • ovary 8-grooved
  • often has a dark spot on each petal (location depending on subspecies)
  • found everywhere

davy’s clarkia
Clarkia davyi

  • sepals all coming free or staying fused in pairs
  • ovary 8-grooved
  • stem prostrate to decumbent
  • petals 5–11 mm, no darker spot
  • winecup clarkia (Clarkia purpurea) has either much larger petals or an erect stem and more acute leaves
  • rare: coastal
ruby chalice clarkia
Clarkia rubicunda
ruby chalice clarkia
Clarkia rubicunda
  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary and immature fruit 4-grooved
  • petals with ± red spot or zone at base of petal
  • petals 10–30 mm
  • stigma exserted beyond anthers
  • found everywhere

presidio clarkia
Clarkia franciscana

  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary and immature fruit 4-grooved
  • petals with ± red spot or zone at base of petal
  • petals 5–13 mm
  • stigma not exserted beyond anthers
  • Redwood Regional Park

farewell to spring
Clarkia amoena

  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary and immature fruit 4-grooved
  • petals with red spot or marks near middle of petal
  • Sunol, scattered in the Santa Cruz range, Marin
chaparral clarkia
Clarkia affinis
chaparral clarkia
Clarkia affinis
  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary and immature fruit 8-grooved
  • scattered around the east bay; only historically on the peninsula

Standard corolla shapes, buds pendent

slender clarkia
Clarkia gracilis

  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary 4-grooved or (rarely) shallowly 8-grooved
  • petals entire, pink-lavender, often lighter toward base, may have red spot near middle or base
  • anthers alike in size and color
  • scattered in Diablo range

canyon clarkia
Clarkia epilobioides

  • all 4 sepals fused or fused in pairs
  • ovary cylindric, not obviously grooved
  • petals entire, white, not flecked, fading pink
  • anthers in 2 unlike series, inner smaller, paler
  • rare: Henry Coe, Sierra Azul, and maybe Rancho San Antonio

waltham creek clarkia
Clarkia modesta

  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary 8-grooved
  • petals entire, pink, generally darker flecked
  • anthers in 2 unlike series, inner smaller, paler
  • scattered in Diablo range

twolobe clarkia
Clarkia biloba ssp. biloba

  • all 4 sepals staying fused
  • ovary 8-grooved
  • petals 2-lobed, lavender to bright pink or magenta, often red-flecked
  • anthers in 2 unlike series, inner smaller, paler
  • Mt. Diablo
