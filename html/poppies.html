poppies

Eschscholzia spp.

The california poppy has a distinct pink disc below the corolla. It is by far the most common poppy in the bay area.

The tufted poppy has no more than an indistinct disc below the corolla. Its recepticle slants smoothly down to the width of the stem. This poppy can be found at Pinnacles NP, but is extremely rare in the bay area.

CalPhotos: recepticle
The diamond-petaled california poppy (Eschscholzia rhombipetala) also has no more than an indistinct disc below the corolla. Its recepticle looks comparatively bloated, as it is wide at the top before slanting down to the width of the stem. This poppy can be found east of Mt. Diablo, but is unknown in the rest of the bay area.

No other poppies are known to grow wild in the bay area in modern times.

Other poppies

The wind poppy is in a different genus in the poppy family. Its corolla and leaves look different from Eschscholzia.

The bush poppy is in a different genus in the poppy family. It grows on a large bush, so it cannot be mistaken for Eschscholzia.
